• All concussions are serious.

    A concussion is a type of traumatic brain injury that causes chemical changes in the brain and sometimes results in stretching and damaging of the brain cells. Learn more

  • Football Quick Tip

    Tackling is responsible for almost two-thirds of concussions in high school football.
    Tip: Limit the amount of contact during practices, including scrimmages and full-speed drills. Learn more

  • Football Quick Tip

    Almost half of concussions in high school football happen during running plays.
    Tip: The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends certified athletic trainers be available at games and practices. Learn more

  • Baseball Quick Tip

    About 1 in 4 concussions in high school baseball result from a player being hit by the pitch.
    Tip: Make sure athletes always wear a batting helmet that fits well and is in good condition. Learn more

  • Soccer Quick Tip

    Concussions in high school soccer most commonly occur when an athlete is heading the ball.
    Tip: US Soccer recommends no heading for any athletes age 10 and under and to limit heading only to practices for athletes ages 11 to 13. Learn more

  • Softball Quick Tip

    Over quarter of concussions happen when an athlete is fielding a batted ball.
    Tip: Teach athletes proper fielding techniques and ways to avoid collisions with other athletes. Learn more

  • Hockey Quick Tip

    Almost two-thirds of concussions in high school ice hockey result from contact with another athlete.
    Tip: Limit the amount of contact during practices, including scrimmages and full-speed drills. Learn more

  • Hockey Quick Tip

    About 1 in 3 concussions in high school ice hockey result from being checked.
    Tip: The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends limiting body checking in competition to athletes age 15 and over. Learn more

  • Basketball Quick Tip

    Most concussions among high school basketball players result from contact with another athlete.
    Tip: Enforce the rules of the sport for fair play, safety, and sportsmanship Learn more

  • Create a Safe Sport Culture.

    Athletes thrive when they have fun playing their sport and receive positive messages from their coaches and parents about reporting concussion symptoms. Learn more

  • Playing or practicing with a concussion is dangerous.

    While an athlete’s brain is still healing, he or she is much more likely to have another concussion putting them at risk for a more serious brain injury or even death. Learn more

  • Concussions affect each athlete differently.

    Most athletes with a concussion feel better within a couple of weeks. However, for some, symptoms may last longer. Learn more
